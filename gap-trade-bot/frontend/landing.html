Trading Knowledge Base

Learn to trade with confidence

Whether you're brand new or refining your edge, these guides cover the fundamentals every trader needs to know.

What is Day Trading?

Day trading is the practice of buying and selling financial instruments within the same trading day. All positions are closed before the market closes, eliminating overnight risk.

  • Profit from intraday price movements — up or down
  • Requires real-time Level 2 data and fast execution
  • Most activity occurs in the first and last hours of the session
  • Pattern Day Trader rule: accounts under $25k are limited to 3 round trips per 5 days

Key Day Trading Strategies

Momentum Trading

Buy stocks with strong upward momentum and high volume; exit when momentum stalls.

Scalping

Make dozens of small trades capturing tiny price movements throughout the day.

Breakout Trading

Enter when price breaks through a key resistance level on elevated volume.

Reversal Trading

Identify overextended moves and trade the snapback — high risk, high reward.

Essential Day Trading Metrics

1–2%

What is Swing Trading?

Swing trading captures multi-day price moves (typically 2–14 days) within a larger trend. Traders hold positions overnight and through weekends, targeting larger percentage gains than day traders.

  • No PDT rule restrictions — can trade with any account size
  • Less screen time than day trading — check charts once or twice daily
  • Targets 5–20%+ moves over several days to weeks
  • Exposed to overnight gap risk — always use a stop

Finding Swing Setups

Trend Following

Buy pullbacks in a strong uptrend at key moving averages (20 EMA, 50 SMA).

Flag & Pennant Breakouts

After a strong move, price consolidates briefly then breaks out with renewed volume.

Earnings Momentum

Stocks that gap up on strong earnings often continue higher over the next few weeks.

Sector Rotation

Follow money rotating into leading sectors — trade the strongest stocks in hot groups.

What is Gap Trading?

A gap occurs when a stock opens significantly higher (gap-up) or lower (gap-down) than its previous close, leaving a visible "gap" on the chart. Gap-ups driven by catalysts like earnings or major news are among the highest-probability setups in trading.

  • Breakaway gap: Signals start of a new trend — most powerful type
  • Continuation gap: Occurs mid-trend, confirms the direction
  • Exhaustion gap: Late in a trend — signals possible reversal
  • Volume is the most important confirmation factor for any gap

Moving Averages

Smooth price data to identify trend direction. The 9 EMA tracks short-term momentum; 20 EMA is a common swing entry zone; 50 & 200 SMA define major support/resistance.

Golden Cross: 50 SMA crosses above 200 SMA — bullish long-term signal

Death Cross: 50 SMA crosses below 200 SMA — bearish signal

RSI — Relative Strength Index

Oscillator (0–100) measuring speed and magnitude of price changes. Values above 70 suggest overbought conditions; below 30 suggests oversold.

Divergence: Price makes new high but RSI doesn't — potential reversal signal

Gap plays: RSI above 70 on open often signals continued momentum

MACD

Moving Average Convergence Divergence — shows momentum and trend direction using the relationship between two EMAs (typically 12 & 26 period).

Signal line cross: MACD crossing above signal line — bullish entry trigger

Histogram: Expanding bars = strengthening momentum

Support & Resistance

Price levels where buying (support) or selling (resistance) pressure repeatedly emerges. Breakouts above resistance or below support are high-probability trade signals.

Role reversal: Broken resistance becomes new support

Round numbers: $50, $100, $500 act as psychological levels

Bollinger Bands

Three bands around a 20 SMA: middle (SMA), upper (+2 std dev), lower (−2 std dev). Bands widen during high volatility and contract during consolidation ("squeeze").

Band walk: Price hugging upper band = strong uptrend

Squeeze breakout: Tight bands often precede explosive moves

Volume Analysis

Volume confirms price moves. A breakout on 5–10× average volume is far more reliable than one on light volume. It reveals whether institutions are participating.

RVOL: Relative volume — current vs average at same time of day

Climax volume: Massive spike often marks exhaustion of a move

Position Sizing & Risk Rules

The 1–2% Rule

Never risk more than 1–2% of your total account on a single trade. On a $10k account that's $100–$200 max loss per trade.

Stop-Loss Placement

Set stops at a technically meaningful level (below a key support or VWAP), not a round dollar amount.

Reward-to-Risk Ratio

Only enter trades where the potential reward is at least 2× the risk. This means you can be right less than 50% of the time and still profit.

Daily Loss Limit

Set a maximum daily loss (e.g. 3–5% of account). When hit, stop trading for the day — protect your capital and mindset.

Trading Psychology

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

Chasing stocks that have already moved is a leading cause of losses. If you missed the entry, wait for the next setup.

Revenge Trading

After a loss, the urge to win it back quickly leads to oversized, undisciplined trades. Always step back after a loss.

Trade Journal

Log every trade — entry, exit, size, reason, and emotion. Patterns in your mistakes become obvious over time.

Process Over Outcome

A losing trade that followed your plan is a success. A winning trade that broke your rules is a failure. Judge process, not P&L.

Pre-Market & After-Hours

Pre-market (4–9:30 AM ET) and after-hours (4–8 PM ET) sessions have lower liquidity and wider spreads. Price moves made overnight often set the tone for the regular session — this is where gap-ups form.

Market Open Volatility

The first 30–60 minutes after open (9:30–10:30 AM ET) are the most volatile and liquid. Most gap trades play out in this window. Experienced traders use this for their best setups; beginners often use it for their worst.

Liquidity & Spreads

Liquidity determines how easily you can enter and exit. High-volume stocks have tight bid/ask spreads (pennies). Low-float, high-momentum stocks can have wider spreads — factor this into your risk calculation.

Market Cap Categories

Large cap (>$10B): Slow movers, less volatile.
Mid cap ($2–10B): Balanced volatility and liquidity.
Small/micro cap (<$2B): High volatility, gap-up candidates, but higher risk and lower liquidity.

Sector Rotation

Institutional money rotates between sectors based on economic cycles. Trade with the money flow — when tech is hot, gap-ups in tech names carry more follow-through than ones in lagging sectors.

Key Market Events

Earnings season (Jan, Apr, Jul, Oct) creates the most gap-up opportunities. Fed announcements (FOMC), CPI releases, and jobs reports cause market-wide volatility — avoid holding positions through major macro events.
